Systems Design Global Markets Self-Service

Dyson Support Home

Transforming fragmented support flows into a unified self-service ecosystem by designing a centralized entry point for 6 global markets—without building new backend capabilities.

Duration
2 weeks
Team
1 Designer (lead), PMs, Engineers
Scope
ANZ → APAC expansion
Status
Live across markets
Year
2022

00 Overview

The Moment & The Brief

Dyson's first Support Home page, launching across Australia & New Zealand with a path to all APAC markets. Users struggled to navigate disconnected support flows. The mission: centralize the entry point and reduce the friction between a product problem and self-service help.

Users lost in fragmented flows

8+ clicks to reach an owner support page from existing entry points. Support was scattered across product pages, guides, parts catalogs, and troubleshooting—with no connective tissue.

Unified support entry

Centralized Support Home became the single pane of glass. Reused existing flows (no new backend) and established a scalable model that's now rolling across SG, KR, IN, TR.

Lead Product Designer

Accountable for end-to-end UX from research & IA to testing, delivery, and regional rollout measurement. Worked inside hard platform constraints—no new backend capabilities allowed.

Key Responsibilities

  • Led end-to-end UX (research → concept → testing → delivery)
  • Defined product direction for 6 global markets
  • Collaborated with content strategist and regional teams
  • Delivered within "no new backend" engineering constraint

01 Context & Constraints

Why This Mattered

Self-service isn't a nice-to-have—it's the brand promise. Premium owners expect premium care. A confusing support flow undercuts the product story we tell at purchase and erodes trust.

Owner Trust

Premium Experience

A fragmented support journey contradicts the premium positioning. Owners shouldn't have to hunt for help on a £300+ product.

Cost to Serve

Reduce Avoidable Contacts

Every failed self-serve attempt becomes a phone call. Reducing friction protects the cost-to-serve ratio across APAC, which matters when you're the smallest M2 market.

Platform Reality

Six markets. Six different support setups. Before designing, I audited what each M2 market actually offered. The picture was fragmented—every region had reinvented support locally. Only the UK had a Support Home and Guides path. Every APAC market was missing both.

The Constraint

No New Backend Capabilities

Can't build new functionality on M2. Can't add new fields, APIs, or database changes. This constraint shaped everything.

The Opportunity

Reuse Existing Flows

Troubleshooting, Spare Parts, Service Centers already existed. The win wasn't new pages—it was the connective tissue.

02 Clarify Problems

Reframing the Brief

The brief arrived as a page layout problem. I reframed it as a behavior problem—one solvable inside platform constraints by reorganizing existing modules and copy, not building new ones.

What Was Briefed

"Design a Support Home page for Australia and New Zealand"

A page-shaped problem, scoped as a content layout exercise.

What I Reframed It As

"How do owners enter self-serve, and where are we losing them?"

A behavior-shaped problem—solvable inside platform constraints by reorganizing existing modules and copy.

03 Shape Concept

Three Mental Models for Support

Each option made a different bet on how owners think when something goes wrong. I explored three models, tested them, and made trade-off decisions based on platform constraints.

Option A: Service First ✓ Shipped

Pick the type of help, then the product.

Make complex navigation simple without rebuilding the system. Buildable inside existing M2 modules. No new components, no engineering blockers. Trade-off: Does not solve the deeper Parts findability problem.

Low Effort · SUS 80.25

Option B: Range Level

Pick the product family, then the help.

Familiar e-commerce pattern applied to support. Reuses existing Product Page IA. Trade-off: Forces a category mental model on services. Sub-category switching broke when owners weren't sure what category their machine was in.

Medium Effort · SUS 80.45

Option C: SKU Level

Pick the exact product, then the help.

Tested well in UK. Best task-completion (9/10) and highest SUS score. Best long-term experience for owners. Trade-off: Requires deep changes to Spare Parts, Accessories, and Troubleshooting flows — a heavy platform build.

High Effort · SUS 84.75

The Decision: The Lightest Model Unlocked the Heaviest One

Testing confirmed owners don't think in services—they think in products. Option C scored highest (SUS 84.75). But with 2 weeks and a hard "no new backend" constraint, Service-first was the launch decision: it shipped inside existing M2 modules, fixed the connective tissue immediately, and bought the evidence and momentum needed to justify the SKU-level build as the long-term north star.

04 Test & Ship

What Usability Testing Told Us to Fix First

Before shipping, we tested the concept with real owners across ANZ. The findings shaped the final design—and revealed where we were wrong about the mental model.

Key Finding: Owners Don't Think in Services

SUS testing across the three models told a clear story: Service-first scored 80.25, Range-level 80.45, and SKU-level 84.75. Owners think in products—"I have a V11"—not in service categories.

This set the long-term direction (SKU-level), while the impact-vs-effort call kept Service-first as the ship-now decision.

Tightening the Journey Before Launch

What we thought: if owners couldn't find machine registration at the right moment, adoption would fail. What's true: owners ignored it on first scroll—registration only matters after they've found their product.

What we thought: "Owner's Guides" vs "User's Manual" was an obvious distinction. What's true: owners couldn't decode the difference and gave up choosing. We merged them into one clear path.

The Shipped Support Home

One entry point, two clear layers: Product Support (Guides & Manuals, Spare Parts, Troubleshooting, Registration) and Other Services (Demo Stores, Repairs, Delivery)—consistent across desktop and mobile.

05 Impact & Reflection

Transforming the "Dead-End" Support Journey

Two months post-launch — every metric moving the right way. Exit rates dropped, self-serve attempts increased, and the model became the template for regional rollout.

What Worked

Centering the owner's mental model, not the business org chart. Owners think "I have this machine" first—not "I need this category of help first." Once we aligned the IA to match that behavior, friction collapsed.

What I'd Do Differently

Push for copy research earlier. The IA was solid, but the first-draft copy was jargony. Testing revealed we should have talked to owners about their language before building the wireframes.

06 Scale

From a Single-Market Page to an APAC System

What we shipped wasn't just a market page — it was a tested template every M2 market could adopt. AU & NZ launched first; Singapore, Korea, India, and Turkey followed, with a clear line of sight to global.

PATTERN 01

Information Hierarchy

Product Support / Other Services — one consistent two-layer structure every market inherits, instead of reinventing support locally.

PATTERN 02

Component Library

Service card · Product picker · Owner block — codified modules so each rollout is assembly, not redesign.

PATTERN 03

Copy & Wayfinding Rules

Specific verbs, no dead-ends — language rules that keep every localized page actionable and every journey with a next step.

THE STRATEGY THAT SCALED

Instead of rebuilding the full platform, we targeted the moments with the most user pain and the clearest opportunity to ship. The "smallest market test bed" became the template for global self-service — AU & NZ → SG · KR · IN · TR.
